The Allais Paradox (Maurice Allais, 1953) shows that people systematically violate Expected Utility Theory. You will face two separate choice problems. Most people pick a combination that is mathematically inconsistent with any well-behaved utility function. See whether your choices follow expected value — or reveal something deeper about how you reason under risk.

The Paradox Explained

Why it happens: People overweight certainty. The jump from 99% → 100% feels far more significant than 10% → 11% — even though both are a 1-percentage-point increase. This "certainty effect" is a cornerstone of Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979).

The Ellsberg Paradox (Daniel Ellsberg, 1961) distinguishes risk (known probabilities) from ambiguity (unknown probabilities). An urn contains 90 balls: exactly 30 are red, and the remaining 60 are some mix of black and yellow — but you are not told the ratio. You will face two bets. Most people's choices reveal ambiguity aversion — a systematic preference for known odds over unknown ones.

The Paradox Explained

Why it happens: People are ambiguity averse — they dislike uncertainty about probabilities themselves, separate from uncertainty about outcomes. This violates Savage's Sure-Thing Principle and Subjective Expected Utility Theory.

Real-world relevance: Ambiguity aversion explains under-investment in unfamiliar asset classes, reluctance to adopt novel business strategies, and why people pay a premium for transparent, well-defined insurance policies over opaque ones.
